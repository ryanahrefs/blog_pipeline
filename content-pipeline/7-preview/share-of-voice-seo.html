What Is Share of Voice in SEO? (How to Measure & Improve It)

Knowing where you rank for individual keywords is useful. But knowing how much of the entire market you're capturing? That's where competitive intelligence gets interesting.

Most SEOs focus on tracking rankings for a handful of important keywords. They celebrate when positions improve and worry when they drop. But this keyword-by-keyword view misses the bigger picture: your overall competitive position in search.

That's where share of voice (SOV) comes in.

Share of voice shows you the percentage of total search visibility you own compared to your competitors. Instead of asking "where do I rank for this keyword?", SOV answers "how much of this market do I control?"

In this guide, you'll learn exactly what SEO share of voice is, why it matters for your business, and how to track it. We'll walk through how to measure SOV using Ahrefs Rank Tracker, which calculates this metric automatically across all your tracked keywords.

What Is Share of Voice in SEO?

Share of voice in SEO measures the percentage of total organic clicks you capture for a set of keywords compared to your competitors.

The concept originally comes from paid advertising, where SOV measured your share of total ad impressions in a market. In the organic search context, it applies the same principle to measure visibility in search engine results.

Think of it this way: if you're tracking 100 keywords in your industry and there are 50,000 total monthly clicks available across those keywords, your share of voice represents how many of those clicks go to your site.

The SOV Formula

Ahrefs calculates share of voice using this formula:

SOV = Total clicks to your site from tracked keywords / Total clicks to all SERP results for those keywords

This click-based calculation is more accurate than simply counting ranking positions because it accounts for:

  • Search volume differences between keywords
  • Click-through rate variations by position
  • The impact of SERP features that shift clicks around

A Simple Example

Let's say you're a coffee equipment retailer tracking 50 keywords related to coffee makers. Here's how SOV might break down:

Site Estimated Clicks Share of Voice
coffeegeek.com 8,500 28%
seriouseats.com 6,200 21%
yoursite.com 4,800 16%
nytimes.com/wirecutter 4,200 14%
Other sites 6,300 21%

Your 16% SOV tells you that out of every 100 clicks for your tracked keywords, roughly 16 go to your site.

SOV Is Not the Same as Ranking Position

This distinction matters. You could rank #1 for ten low-volume keywords and still have lower SOV than a competitor ranking #3-5 for a few high-volume terms.

A site ranking #2 for "best coffee maker" (40,000 monthly searches) likely captures more traffic than a site ranking #1 for "single-cup pour-over coffee maker" (800 monthly searches).

SOV accounts for this by weighting keyword importance based on actual click potential.

Why Does SEO Share of Voice Matter?

Tracking individual keyword rankings tells you something. Tracking share of voice tells you everything you need to know about your competitive position.

SOV Predicts Market Share Growth

Here's the most compelling reason to care about SOV: research shows a strong correlation between share of voice and market share growth.

The concept of "Excess Share of Voice" (ESOV), studied extensively by Les Binet and Peter Field, describes what happens when your visibility exceeds your current market share:

  • ESOV > 0: Your share of voice exceeds your market share. You're likely to grow.
  • ESOV < 0: Your share of voice is below your market share. You're likely to decline.

The research found specific correlations: in B2C markets, a 10% ESOV leads to an annual market share growth of 0.7%, while B2B markets show similar results with 0.6% annual growth.

For example, if your business holds 15% market share but you have 25% share of voice in search, you have +10% ESOV. That excess visibility typically converts to market share growth over time.

This makes SOV a leading indicator. While traffic and revenue tell you what happened, SOV hints at what's likely to happen next.

SOV Provides Competitive Context

Individual keyword rankings lack context. Sure, you rank #4 for your target keyword—but is that good or bad relative to your competitors?

SOV answers this by showing exactly where you stand. You might rank well for 30 keywords, but if a competitor dominates the other 70 you're tracking, your overall competitive position is weaker than individual rankings suggest.

SOV Reveals Opportunities and Threats

Tracking SOV over time reveals trends that individual ranking reports miss. In Ahrefs Rank Tracker, the SOV trend charts (added in January 2025) show whether your competitive position is strengthening or weakening.

Watch for these signals:

  • Declining SOV: Early warning that competitors are gaining ground
  • Rising competitor SOV: An emerging threat worth investigating
  • Flat SOV despite content efforts: Your strategy may need adjustment
  • Sudden SOV spike (competitor): They may have launched new content or earned significant links

SOV Is a Better Reporting Metric

For executives and stakeholders who don't live in SEO data, share of voice communicates more effectively than ranking reports.

"We increased our share of voice from 18% to 24% this quarter" is more meaningful than "we improved rankings for 47 keywords." SOV translates technical SEO progress into competitive positioning language that business leaders understand.

Share of Voice vs. Related Metrics

SOV is one of several metrics that measure search performance. Here's how it relates to others:

Metric What It Measures Best For
Share of Voice % of clicks you capture vs. total available Competitive positioning
Search Visibility % of SERPs where you appear Coverage assessment
Organic Traffic Actual visitors to your site Performance measurement
Keyword Rankings Your position for specific terms Individual keyword tracking
Market Share % of revenue/customers in your market Business performance

SOV vs. Traffic: An Important Distinction

Share of voice measures potential visibility. Traffic measures actual visitors.

You could have high SOV but relatively low traffic if your click-through rates are poor. Maybe your title tags don't compel clicks, or competitors have rich snippets that draw attention away from your standard results.

The relationship between SOV and traffic reveals optimization opportunities:

  • High SOV, low traffic: Improve titles, meta descriptions, and CTR
  • Low SOV, decent traffic: You're converting well but need more visibility
  • High SOV, high traffic: You're winning—protect this position

Track both metrics. Use SOV for competitive benchmarking and trend analysis. Use traffic for actual performance measurement.

How to Measure SEO Share of Voice

You can calculate SOV manually, but tools make tracking much easier—especially when monitoring competitors over time.

Manual Calculation (Quick Method)

For a rough SOV estimate without tools:

  1. List your target keywords (start with 20-50 core terms)
  2. Get search volume for each keyword
  3. Check your ranking position for each
  4. Estimate your CTR based on position

According to First Page Sage's 2026 CTR study, average CTRs by position are approximately:

  • Position 1: ~40%
  • Position 2: ~19%
  • Position 3: ~10%
  • Position 4-10: ~2-7%
  • Position 11+: <2%

Note: Recent studies show CTRs have declined significantly with AI Overviews, with position #1 CTRs dropping from 28% to 19% in some measurements—a 32% decline from 2024 to 2025.

  1. Calculate estimated clicks: search volume x CTR
  2. Sum your estimated clicks across all keywords
  3. Divide by total search volume to get your SOV %

This works for quick estimates, but has limitations:

  • Time-consuming to update regularly
  • Doesn't account for SERP features that affect CTR
  • Hard to track multiple competitors consistently
  • CTR estimates are rough averages that vary by query type

Tips for Accurate SOV Tracking

Be selective with keywords. Track keywords relevant to your business goals. A tool company tracking 1,000 generic "SEO" keywords will get noisy data. Track 100 high-intent keywords related to your specific product category for cleaner insights.

Tag and segment keywords. Rank Tracker supports tagging, which lets you analyze SOV for subsets of keywords. Segment by:

  • Topic/product category
  • Funnel stage (awareness, consideration, decision)
  • Priority level
  • Search intent type

Update your keyword set. Add new keywords as your content strategy expands. Remove keywords that become irrelevant. SOV is only meaningful if your tracked keywords reflect your actual competitive landscape.

Compare apples to apples. Ensure competitors are truly competing for the same terms. A competitor might have high overall domain authority but not actually target your keywords.

How to Increase Your SEO Share of Voice

Improving SOV comes down to ranking better for more of the keywords that matter. Here are six approaches:

1. Expand Your Keyword Coverage

If competitors rank for keywords you don't target, they're capturing clicks you could win.

Use Ahrefs' Content Gap tool to find keywords where competitors rank but you don't. These represent direct opportunities to expand your SOV through new content.

Steps:

  1. Enter your domain and 2-3 competitors in Content Gap
  2. Filter for keywords where competitors rank but you don't
  3. Sort by traffic potential to prioritize
  4. Create content targeting these gap keywords

Even modest rankings (#5-10) on new keywords add to your SOV. You don't need to win #1 for everything—just capture a share of more keywords.

2. Improve Rankings for High-Volume Keywords

Position improvements on high-volume keywords have outsized impact on SOV.

Moving from position #5 to #3 on a keyword with 50,000 monthly searches affects SOV more than ranking #1 for ten keywords with 500 searches each.

In Keywords Explorer, sort by Traffic Potential to identify which keywords will have the biggest impact on your SOV when improved. Focus optimization efforts there.

Priority improvements:

  • Page 2 to page 1 (biggest CTR jump)
  • Bottom of page 1 to top 5
  • Position 4-5 to position 1-3

3. Capture More SERP Real Estate

SERP features like featured snippets, "People Also Ask" boxes, and image carousels contribute to visibility beyond standard organic rankings.

According to research from Search Engine Watch, featured snippets can have click-through rates of around 35-43%, making them valuable for SOV. If you win that snippet, your SOV for that keyword increases significantly—even if your organic ranking stays the same.

Use the SERP Overview in Keywords Explorer to see what SERP features appear for your target keywords and which competitors are capturing them. Then optimize your content for those formats:

  • Featured snippets: Clear definitions, step-by-step lists, concise tables
  • PAA boxes: Answer common questions directly in your content
  • Image carousels: Optimize images with descriptive alt text and filenames

4. Improve Click-Through Rates

CTR affects SOV because SOV is calculated based on clicks, not just rankings.

At the same ranking position, better click-through rates mean more clicks and higher SOV. Improvements to title tags and meta descriptions pay dividends here:

  • Include your primary keyword naturally
  • Use numbers and specific data when relevant
  • Add power words that create curiosity or urgency
  • Match the search intent clearly
  • Differentiate from competitors' titles visible in the same SERP

Test titles on high-volume pages. Even a 10% CTR improvement on an important page moves your SOV.

5. Build Topical Authority

Sites that own a topic tend to rank across many related keywords. Building topical authority creates a rising-tide effect that lifts all your keyword rankings in that area.

Create comprehensive topic coverage:

  • Identify a core topic where you want to compete
  • Map all the subtopics and questions related to that topic
  • Create content addressing each subtopic
  • Interlink content to create clear topical relationships

As search engines recognize your authority on a topic, rankings improve across the cluster—and SOV follows.

6. Monitor and Respond to Competitors

SOV is a competitive metric. Your position depends not just on what you do, but what competitors do.

Rank Tracker's Competitors Overview shows when a competitor's SOV is rising. When you spot an upward trend, investigate what's driving it. Use Site Explorer to check:

  • Are they publishing new content?
  • Have they earned significant new backlinks?
  • Did they make technical improvements?

Understanding their playbook helps you respond effectively. Sometimes the right response is to compete directly. Sometimes it's to find a different angle where they're not focused.

The Rise of AI Share of Voice

As AI-powered search experiences grow (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews), a new dimension of share of voice is emerging: AI SOV.

What Is AI Share of Voice?

AI share of voice measures your brand's visibility in AI-generated responses. When someone asks ChatGPT for product recommendations or uses Perplexity to research a topic, does your brand appear in the response?

Similar to traditional SOV, AI SOV asks: what percentage of AI responses in your category mention or cite your brand compared to competitors?

Why It Matters

AI search is growing rapidly. Some users now start research in ChatGPT or Perplexity rather than Google. If your brand doesn't appear in those AI-generated answers, you're invisible to a growing segment of searchers.

Being cited in AI responses also reinforces brand awareness. Even if users don't click through immediately, repeated brand mentions build recognition.

How Ahrefs Tracks AI SOV

Ahrefs' Brand Radar monitors your brand mentions across AI systems like ChatGPT and Perplexity. It tracks:

  • Total impressions where your brand appears in AI responses
  • Share of voice compared to competitors in AI systems
  • Which queries and topics trigger your brand mentions

This gives you visibility into the emerging AI search channel alongside traditional organic SOV. As AI search grows, tracking both metrics provides a complete picture of your brand's search visibility.

Connecting Traditional SOV to AI SOV

Interestingly, strong organic visibility often correlates with AI citations. AI systems frequently reference content that ranks well in traditional search, since that content is typically well-researched and authoritative.

This means efforts to improve traditional SOV often support AI SOV as well. Creating comprehensive, well-sourced content that ranks well also positions you to be cited by AI systems.

Common SOV Mistakes to Avoid

1. Tracking Too Many Keywords

More keywords doesn't mean better SOV data. If you track 1,000 keywords including many irrelevant ones, your SOV percentage becomes noisy and less meaningful.

Focus on 50-200 keywords that genuinely matter to your business. Better to have accurate SOV for the right keywords than inflated or deflated SOV from including everything.

2. Ignoring Competitor Context

SOV as an absolute number means little. Is 15% SOV good? Bad? It depends entirely on your competitors.

Always benchmark against specific competitors. Track at least 3-5 relevant competitors so you understand whether your 15% SOV means you're winning, losing, or holding steady in your competitive landscape.

3. Focusing Only on SOV, Ignoring Traffic

SOV is a leading indicator. Traffic is the lagging indicator that actually matters.

High SOV with low conversion means you're capturing visibility but not turning it into business results. Watch both:

  • Use SOV to measure competitive visibility
  • Use traffic and conversions to measure business impact

If SOV is high but traffic isn't following, investigate CTR issues or keyword intent mismatches.

4. Not Segmenting Keywords

Overall SOV can mask important differences. You might have 25% SOV overall, but when you segment:

  • Informational keywords: 40% SOV
  • Commercial keywords: 10% SOV

That's a problem. You're capturing awareness-stage searches but losing where it matters for revenue.

Use Rank Tracker's tagging feature to segment keywords by topic, funnel stage, or priority. This reveals whether you're dominating informational queries but losing on commercial ones—or vice versa.
