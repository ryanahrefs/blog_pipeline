# Keyword Gap Analysis: How to Find and Fill the Gaps in Your SEO Strategy

**Target Keyword**: keyword gap
**Word Count**: 2,487
**Status**: Cited Draft
**Source Draft**: ./4-drafts/keyword-gap.md

---

Most websites rank for only a fraction of the keywords their competitors do. If your competitor ranks for 5,000 keywords and you rank for 1,000, that's 4,000 potential opportunities you're missing.

Keyword gap analysis shows you exactly which keywords your competitors are winning that you're not even competing for. But finding gaps is only half the battle. The real skill is knowing which gaps to fill and how.

In this guide, I'll show you how to find keyword gaps using Ahrefs, prioritize the opportunities that actually matter, and create a plan to fill those gaps.

## What is a keyword gap?

A keyword gap is a keyword that your competitors rank for but you don't. It represents traffic you're missing out on.

The simplest way to think about it: if Competitor A ranks #3 for "SEO audit checklist" (1,500 monthly searches) and you don't appear anywhere in the top 100, that's a keyword gap. They're getting that traffic. You're not.

Keyword gap analysis is the process of systematically finding these gaps across all the keywords your competitors rank for. When you run this analysis, you often discover hundreds or thousands of keywords you never knew you were missing.

You'll sometimes hear this called "content gap analysis" too. The terms get used interchangeably, but there's a subtle difference.

### Keyword gap vs content gap

A keyword gap focuses on specific search terms you're missing. A [content gap](https://ahrefs.com/blog/content-gap-analysis/) is broader - it's about topics or content pieces you don't have at all.

In practice, fixing keyword gaps usually means creating content, so the distinction doesn't matter much. If you're not ranking for "how to do a content audit," you probably need to create a content audit guide. The keyword gap reveals the content gap.

Ahrefs uses "Content Gap" as the tool name, but it finds keyword gaps. Don't let the terminology confuse you.

## Why keyword gap analysis matters

Your competitors have already validated which keywords are worth targeting. Gap analysis lets you steal their playbook.

Here's why I run gap analysis for every SEO project:

**It saves research time.** Instead of brainstorming keywords from scratch, you find proven winners. If three competitors all rank for "email marketing automation," that keyword has already been validated as worthwhile.

**It reveals content opportunities.** Each gap suggests content you could create. A gap report with 500 keywords is essentially a content calendar waiting to be prioritized.

**It benchmarks your performance.** Seeing how many keywords competitors rank for (versus you) gives you a clear picture of where you stand. If they rank for 3x more keywords, you know there's work to do.

**It prioritizes effort.** Not all keywords matter equally. Gap analysis helps you focus resources on keywords that are actually driving traffic for competitors, not theoretical opportunities from a keyword research tool.

## How to find keyword gaps with Ahrefs

The fastest way to find keyword gaps is with Ahrefs' [Content Gap tool](https://ahrefs.com/content-gap). Here's the exact process I use.

### Step 1: Identify your SEO competitors

Your SEO competitors aren't always your business competitors.

A local bakery might compete with national recipe blogs for "chocolate chip cookie recipe," even though those blogs aren't selling cookies. For keyword gap analysis, you want to find the sites that rank for your target keywords, not just the companies you compete with for customers.

To find your SEO competitors, go to [Site Explorer](https://ahrefs.com/site-explorer), enter your domain, and check the Organic Competitors report. This shows domains that rank for similar keywords to yours, sorted by how much keyword overlap you share.

[SCREENSHOT: Site Explorer Organic Competitors report showing competitor domains with keyword overlap percentages]

Pick 3-5 direct competitors. More than that dilutes the results. You want competitors who are similar enough that their keywords would actually be relevant to your site.

For example, if you run an SEO blog, comparing against Moz, Search Engine Journal, and Backlinko makes sense. Comparing against a general marketing agency that happens to rank for a few SEO terms doesn't.

### Step 2: Run the Content Gap analysis

In Site Explorer, click "Content Gap" in the left sidebar.

Add your domain in the top field, then add up to 10 competitor domains below. Click "Show keywords" and Ahrefs will return every keyword that at least one competitor ranks for in the top 10 that you don't rank for at all.

[SCREENSHOT: Content Gap tool interface showing domain input fields and results]

The default results can be overwhelming. I've seen gap analyses return 50,000+ keywords. That's why filtering is essential.

### Step 3: Filter the results

Use the "Intersect" filter to narrow results. The options are:

- **1+ competitors**: At least one competitor ranks (default, most results)
- **2+ competitors**: At least two competitors rank (fewer, higher quality)
- **All competitors**: Every competitor you entered ranks (fewest, strongest signals)

I usually start with "All competitors." If all 3-5 of your competitors rank for a keyword, that's a strong signal it's worth pursuing. You can always loosen the filter later.

Next, apply these filters:

- **Position**: Top 10 only. This focuses on keywords competitors actually win, not ones where they rank #47.
- **Volume**: 100+ minimum. This filters out ultra-long-tail terms with minimal traffic.
- **Keyword Difficulty**: Under 30 (or whatever matches your site's authority). If your site has a Domain Rating around 40, filtering for KD < 30 helps you focus on keywords you can realistically rank for.

[SCREENSHOT: Content Gap with filters applied showing refined keyword list]

### Step 4: Export and analyze

Export the filtered keyword list for deeper analysis. I usually export to Google Sheets so I can add columns for prioritization.

In your export, pay attention to the "Parent Topic" column. This groups related keywords together. If you see 15 keywords all with the same parent topic, that's one piece of content that could rank for all of them, not 15 separate articles.

Also check the search intent for each cluster. Are these informational queries (blog content), commercial queries (comparison pages), or transactional queries (product pages)? The content you create needs to match what searchers expect.

## How to prioritize keyword gap opportunities

Finding gaps is easy. Finding the *right* gaps to pursue is where most people fail.

Not every gap is worth filling. Some gaps exist because the keyword doesn't make sense for your site. Others are too competitive. The key is prioritization.

### The three-filter framework

I evaluate every gap keyword against three criteria:

**1. Traffic potential over raw volume**

Don't just look at the search volume of the keyword. Look at the traffic potential column, which shows how much traffic the top-ranking page gets from ALL keywords it ranks for.

A keyword with 500 monthly searches might seem smaller than one with 2,000. But if the top page for the 500-search keyword gets 5,000 total monthly visits, that's the better opportunity.

**2. Difficulty that matches your authority**

If your site has a Domain Rating of 30 and you're targeting a KD 80 keyword, you're fighting a battle you probably won't win. At least not without significant link building.

Match difficulty to your current authority. You can always tackle harder keywords later as your site grows.

**3. Business relevance**

Some keywords have great metrics but don't align with what you sell.

I once found a gap keyword with 10,000 monthly searches and a KD of 15. Perfect on paper. But the keyword was about a feature our product didn't have. Ranking for it would bring traffic that couldn't convert. That's a gap you leave unfilled.

### Quick wins vs strategic bets

Split your gaps into two buckets:

**Quick wins** are keywords where you have high traffic potential, low difficulty, high business relevance, AND you already have related content you could expand. Pursue these first. They're the lowest-hanging fruit.

**Strategic bets** are keywords that require more investment - creating entirely new content, building topical authority first, or waiting until your domain is stronger. Plan these into your roadmap but don't expect immediate results.

## What to do with your keyword gaps

Finding gaps is useless without action. Here's how to turn gaps into rankings.

### Option 1: Optimize existing content

Before creating something new, check if you already have content that should rank but doesn't.

Search your own site: `site:yourdomain.com "keyword"`. If you have a page about the topic that isn't ranking, the problem might be optimization, not content creation.

Common fixes for existing content:
- Add missing subtopics that top-ranking pages cover
- Update outdated information
- Increase depth and word count where thin
- Improve internal linking to the page
- Add better examples and visuals

### Option 2: Create new content

For gaps where you have no relevant content, you'll need to create something new.

Before writing, analyze the top 3-5 ranking pages for your target keyword. Look at:

- **Content format**: Is it a guide, listicle, comparison, or tool? Match the format.
- **Word count**: How long are the top results? You don't need to match exactly, but being significantly shorter is usually a problem.
- **Subtopics**: What sections do all top results include? These are probably required.
- **Unique angles**: What could you add that competitors don't cover?

Create content that matches the search intent and format, but adds something new. Don't just copy what's ranking.

### Option 3: Build supporting content

Some keywords require topical authority to rank. If competitors have 20 articles about email marketing and you have 1, you probably won't outrank them for "email marketing best practices" even with a great article.

For these gaps, build a content cluster. Create supporting articles around the main topic, then internally link them to your target page. Over time, you build the [topical authority](https://ahrefs.com/blog/topical-authority/) needed to rank for competitive terms.

### Track your progress

Add your target gap keywords to Ahrefs' [Rank Tracker](https://ahrefs.com/rank-tracker) to monitor progress. You'll see how your positions change over time after publishing new content or optimizing existing pages.

[SCREENSHOT: Rank Tracker showing keyword positions improving over time]

Set a reminder to re-run your gap analysis quarterly. Competitors publish new content, new keywords emerge, and your own rankings change. A gap you couldn't pursue six months ago might be achievable now.

## Common keyword gap analysis mistakes

Most people fail at gap analysis not because they can't find gaps, but because they target the wrong ones. Here are the mistakes I see most often:

### Mistake 1: Targeting every gap

I've seen sites try to create content for hundreds of gap keywords at once. That's a recipe for thin, mediocre content across the board.

Not all gaps are opportunities. Some keywords are gaps for good reason - they're too competitive, too irrelevant, or too low-value. Focus beats breadth.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring search intent

Finding a gap keyword doesn't mean your existing content can rank for it.

If the gap keyword is "best CRM software" and all top results are comparison articles with pricing tables, your product page won't rank. You need to match the intent with the right content type.

### Mistake 3: Not considering your authority

A DR 30 site targeting keywords where the top 10 results all have DR 80+ is fighting an uphill battle. You might get there eventually, but it's not a quick win.

Build authority progressively. Target easier keywords first, build links and topical authority, then tackle the harder gaps.

### Mistake 4: Running gap analysis once

Your competitors aren't standing still. They're publishing new content, optimizing existing pages, and targeting new keywords.

If you run gap analysis once and never revisit it, you're working with outdated data. Run it quarterly at minimum, monthly if you're in a fast-moving industry.

### Mistake 5: Only looking at direct competitors

Sometimes the best gaps come from adjacent industries.

A B2B software company might find valuable keyword gaps by analyzing content marketing agencies, not just other software companies. They're competing for the same educational keywords even if they're not competing for customers.

## Final thoughts

Keyword gap analysis is one of the fastest ways to find proven keyword opportunities. Instead of guessing which keywords might work, you find keywords that are already working for your competitors.

Use Ahrefs' Content Gap tool to find the gaps, prioritize based on traffic potential, difficulty, and business relevance, then create or optimize content to fill those gaps.

Start with your top 3 competitors and run your first gap analysis today. Even 10 well-prioritized gaps can fuel months of content strategy.
